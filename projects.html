Project 01 · Patient Access

Patient Access Transformation Dashboard

An executive command center for patient access — making scheduling, workforce, referral, and capacity performance visible enough to act on.

1Executive Summary

Patient access is the front door of the health system and the leading indicator of both growth and patient experience. Yet access performance is usually scattered across scheduling systems, contact-center reports, referral logs, and capacity spreadsheets that no single leader sees together. This project designs a single executive dashboard that unifies those signals into one operating view and connects each metric to a decision an operating leader can actually make.

The thesis is simple: when access leaders can see demand, capacity, conversion, and workforce in one place, they stop managing anecdotes and start managing the system.

2Business Challenge

Health systems lose volume, revenue, and goodwill at the access layer in ways that rarely show up clearly until they compound:

  • Third-next-available appointments stretch to weeks while clinical capacity sits unevenly utilized.
  • Referrals leak out of the network because the loop between referral, scheduling, and confirmation is broken.
  • Contact centers miss calls and abandon patients at the exact moment they are trying to get care.
  • Workforce is staffed to averages, not to demand patterns, so service breaks at predictable peaks.

The underlying problem is not a lack of data — it is a lack of an integrated, decision-oriented view.

3Dashboard Design

The dashboard is organized into four operating quadrants, each answering one executive question:

  • Demand & Access — How long until a patient can be seen? (Third-next-available, time-to-appointment, new-patient lag.)
  • Capacity & Utilization — Are we using the capacity we have? (Template utilization, no-show rate, bump/cancellation rate.)
  • Referral Flow — Are referrals converting and staying in network? (Referral-to-appointment conversion, leakage, time-to-schedule.)
  • Workforce & Service — Can patients reach us when they call? (Call abandonment, service level, schedule adherence.)

Each quadrant uses the sage-green accent to flag the single metric most out of tolerance, so the eye goes to the decision, not the decoration.

5Executive Insights

A dashboard like this typically surfaces three patterns that change leadership conversations:

  • The capacity is there; the matching is not. Utilization gaps and long waits usually coexist — an allocation problem, not a volume problem.
  • Leakage is a scheduling-friction problem. Most out-of-network loss traces back to how hard it is to get the first appointment, not patient preference.
  • Abandonment predicts downstream demand. Every abandoned call is deferred or lost care that resurfaces — often more expensively — later.

6Recommendations

  • Stand up a single access dashboard with clear ownership and a weekly operating cadence.
  • Implement demand-based scheduling templates and centralized scheduling for high-leakage specialties.
  • Staff the contact center to demand curves, not headcount averages.
  • Close the referral loop with automated tracking from referral to confirmed appointment.

7Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1 · 0–30 days

See it

Define KPIs, unify data sources, stand up the dashboard, establish ownership and cadence.

Phase 2 · 30–90 days

Move it

Pilot demand-based templates and contact-center staffing; launch referral-loop tracking in two specialties.

Phase 3 · 90–180 days

Scale it

Roll out across service lines, embed in operating reviews, tie metrics to accountability and incentives.

8Leadership Lessons

Dashboards do not improve access; decisions do. The value of this artifact is not the visualization — it is the operating discipline it enables: one view, clear owners, a weekly cadence, and a bias toward action. Technology makes the problem visible; leadership makes it move.

Project 02 · Operations · Lean Six Sigma

Healthcare Operations Transformation Case Study

Reducing time-to-appointment and improving referral conversion — a structured operations diagnosis from current state to financial impact.

1Executive Summary

An ambulatory specialty practice is losing patients to long waits and referral leakage. This case study applies a Lean Six Sigma DMAIC approach to diagnose why, isolate the highest-leverage root causes, and recommend interventions with a modeled financial return. The headline: most of the wait and most of the leakage are created by process friction the organization controls — not by demand it cannot.

2Current State

  • Average time-to-appointment for new patients: ~24 days (illustrative)
  • Referral-to-appointment conversion: ~58% (illustrative)
  • No-show rate: ~12%, concentrated in long-lead-time bookings
  • Scheduling spread across decentralized teams with inconsistent templates and no closed referral loop

The patient journey from referral to visit passes through multiple handoffs, each adding delay and drop-off.

3Root Cause Analysis

Using fishbone and five-whys analysis, the delay and leakage trace to a small set of process drivers:

  • Template mismatch — appointment templates do not reflect real demand by visit type.
  • Manual referral handling — no automated tracking means referrals stall in queues.
  • Decentralized scheduling — inconsistent rules produce uneven access across the same specialty.
  • Long lead times drive no-shows — which then re-inflate the backlog, a reinforcing loop.

4Lean Six Sigma Framework (DMAIC)

ImproveInterventionDemand-based templates, centralized scheduling, automated referral loop, no-show mitigation.

5Recommendations

  • Redesign templates to match demand by visit type and provider.
  • Centralize scheduling for the specialty with standardized rules.
  • Automate the referral loop end-to-end with status tracking and outreach.
  • Deploy reminders, waitlists, and overbooking logic to recover no-show capacity.

8Executive Takeaways

  • Access problems are usually process problems the organization can fix.
  • DMAIC turns frustration into a sequenced, defensible plan.
  • Sustained gains require control mechanisms, not just a one-time project.

Project 03 · Executive Brief

AI in Healthcare Operations — Executive Brief

Where artificial intelligence creates real operating value across workforce, revenue cycle, and patient access — and how to govern it responsibly.

1Executive Summary

AI is moving from pilot to production in healthcare operations, but the value is uneven and the risk is real. This brief frames where AI delivers near-term operating return — workforce optimization, revenue cycle automation, and patient access — and pairs each opportunity with the governance discipline required to deploy it safely. The argument is not "adopt AI." It is "automate the routine, augment the expert, and govern everything."

2Current Challenges

  • Administrative burden and labor cost are consuming margin.
  • Workforce shortages strain access and service.
  • Revenue cycle leakage from denials, manual coding, and rework.
  • Fragmented data and inconsistent processes limit scale.

3AI Opportunities

The practical frontier is automation of high-volume routine work and augmentation of expert judgment — not replacement of clinical or operating leadership.

  • Automate: status checks, eligibility, documentation drafting, routine coding, call triage.
  • Augment: denial prediction, demand forecasting, capacity optimization, risk flagging.
  • Avoid (for now): unsupervised decisions affecting clinical care or patient eligibility without human review.

4Workforce Applications

  • Demand forecasting and AI-assisted scheduling to staff to real patterns.
  • Conversational AI for routine patient and employee inquiries.
  • Knowledge assistants that cut onboarding time and support frontline decisions.

5Revenue Cycle Applications

  • Denial prediction and prevention before claims go out.
  • Automated prior authorization and eligibility verification.
  • Computer-assisted coding and documentation support.
  • Intelligent worklist prioritization for follow-up.

6Patient Access Applications

  • AI-assisted scheduling and intelligent referral routing.
  • Automated appointment reminders and no-show prediction.
  • Self-service access through conversational front doors.

7Governance Considerations

Drawing on direct experience supporting AI governance, responsible deployment requires guardrails built in from the start:

  • Clear use-case approval and risk-tiering process.
  • Human-in-the-loop for any decision touching care or coverage.
  • Bias, accuracy, and drift monitoring with defined ownership.
  • Transparency, documentation, and regulatory alignment.

8Financial Impact (illustrative)

The near-term ROI concentrates in labor-intensive, high-volume administrative work: reduced cost-to-collect, fewer denials and less rework, recovered staff capacity, and improved access throughput. Value should be modeled per use case with a clear baseline before scaling. Add organization-specific ROI modeling before publishing.

9Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1

Govern & prioritize

Stand up AI governance; rank use cases by value and risk; pick two low-risk, high-volume pilots.

Phase 2

Pilot & prove

Deploy with human-in-the-loop, measure against baseline, validate accuracy and ROI.

Phase 3

Scale & monitor

Expand proven use cases; institutionalize monitoring, retraining, and accountability.

10Executive Recommendations

  • Lead with governance — it is the enabler, not the obstacle, of responsible scale.
  • Start where work is routine, high-volume, and low-clinical-risk.
  • Measure every use case against a real baseline before expanding.
  • Keep humans accountable for any decision that reaches a patient.
