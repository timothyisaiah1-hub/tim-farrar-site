How I Would Improve Patient Access at an Academic Medical Center

First draft · ~6 min read · by Timothy Farrar, MHA

If I were handed responsibility for patient access at an academic medical center tomorrow, I would not start with a technology purchase or a reorganization. I would start with a single question: how long does it take a new patient to be seen, and where exactly does that time get created?

The access problem is rarely a capacity problem

Academic medical centers are unusually complex access environments: tertiary and quaternary demand, teaching schedules, research time, and a referral base that spans an entire region. It is tempting to conclude that long waits mean too little capacity. In my experience, the more common truth is that capacity exists but is mismatched to demand — wrong templates, wrong rules, wrong routing.

My first 90 days

I would make access visible before trying to fix it. That means one dashboard showing third-next-available appointments, template utilization, referral conversion, and contact-center service levels — by specialty, every week. You cannot manage what leaders cannot see together.

From there, three moves create most of the early value: redesign scheduling templates to match real demand, centralize scheduling for the highest-leakage specialties, and close the referral loop so no referral stalls in a queue. None of these require new technology. They require operating discipline.

Access, patient experience, and financial performance are the same problem viewed from different seats. Fix the front door and all three improve.

Why this works

Every shortened wait is a recaptured patient, a retained referral, and a better experience — at once. That is the leverage of the access layer, and it is why I would treat it as the first operating priority, not the last.

Outline
  • Hook: the one question that reframes access
  • Myth: access ≠ capacity at an AMC
  • First 90 days: visibility → templates → centralization → referral loop
  • Thesis quote: access/experience/finance are one problem
  • Close: leverage of the front door

Five Metrics Every Healthcare COO Should Monitor

First draft · ~5 min read · by Timothy Farrar, MHA

Operating leaders drown in dashboards. The discipline is not collecting more metrics — it is knowing the few that predict trouble before it arrives. Here are five I would never take my eyes off.

1. Third-next-available appointment

The truest measure of access a patient actually feels. It moves before revenue does and signals demand, capacity, and scheduling health in one number.

2. Referral conversion and network leakage

Every unconverted referral is downstream volume walking out the door. Leakage is one of the most expensive and least-watched losses in healthcare operations.

3. Cost-to-collect and denial rate

The revenue cycle is where operations becomes cash. Rising cost-to-collect or denials is an early warning that process discipline is slipping.

4. Labor productivity vs. demand

Staffing to averages guarantees you are over-resourced in the valleys and broken at the peaks. Productivity measured against real demand is the operating leader's truth serum.

5. Patient experience at the point of access

Experience scores at the front door predict loyalty and growth. They are a leading indicator dressed up as a satisfaction survey.

If these five are healthy, most of the rest tends to follow. If any one is sliding, it is telling you where the next problem will surface.
Outline
  • Hook: too many dashboards, too few signals
  • 5 leading indicators with the "why" for each
  • Close: these five predict the rest

AI and the Future of Healthcare Operations

First draft · ~6 min read · by Timothy Farrar, MHA

The question is no longer whether AI belongs in healthcare operations. It is which work to automate, which to augment, and how to govern all of it without losing the trust the work depends on.

Automate the routine

The clearest near-term value is in high-volume, low-clinical-risk administrative work: eligibility checks, status updates, documentation drafting, routine coding, and call triage. This is where AI returns capacity and cost the fastest.

Augment the expert

For judgment-heavy work — denial prediction, demand forecasting, capacity optimization — AI should make the expert faster and sharper, not replace them. The human stays accountable; the model does the heavy lifting underneath.

Govern everything

Having supported AI governance directly, I am convinced governance is the enabler of scale, not its obstacle. Use-case risk-tiering, human-in-the-loop for anything touching care or coverage, and monitoring for bias and drift are what make responsible scale possible.

Automate the routine, augment the expert, and govern everything. That is the operating doctrine for AI in healthcare.

  • Automate / Augment / Govern framework
  • Governance as enabler
  • Close: the operating doctrine
Transformation

Lessons Learned from Enterprise Transformation

First draft · ~5 min read · by Timothy Farrar, MHA

I have worked inside enterprise-scale change — an Epic implementation, a governance platform rollout reaching tens of thousands of employees, revenue cycle transformation. The technology is rarely the hard part. The alignment is.

People and process come before technology

Every failed go-live I have seen failed on readiness, not software. When people understand the why and own the how, technology lands. When they do not, no platform saves you.

Governance prevents expensive surprises

Testing governance and operational readiness discipline are unglamorous and decisive. They are the difference between a controlled launch and a costly recovery.

Influence beats authority

Enterprise change is led across boundaries you do not control. The core skill is stakeholder influence — earning alignment without relying on org-chart power.

Large-scale transformation succeeds when people, process, and technology are aligned — in that order.
Outline
  • Hook: technology is rarely the hard part
  • People/process before technology
  • Governance prevents surprises
  • Influence over authority
  • Close: alignment in order

Improving Healthcare Through Analytics

First draft · ~5 min read · by Timothy Farrar, MHA

I came up through analytics, and it taught me a hard lesson early: a beautiful dashboard that changes no decision is a cost, not an asset. Analytics earns its keep only when it drives action.

Start from the decision, not the data

The best analytics work begins by naming the decision it must improve. Then you build backward to the metric, the source, and the visualization — never the other way around.

Make the signal impossible to miss

Operating leaders do not have time to hunt. Good dashboards point straight at the one thing out of tolerance and the action it implies.

Close the loop

The measure of an analytics program is not adoption of the tool — it is the operational change it produces. Data only creates value when it drives a decision and a result.

Data only creates value when it drives operational change. Everything else is decoration.
Outline
  • Hook: the dashboard that changed nothing
  • Decision-first design
  • Make the signal obvious
  • Close the loop to action
